While working toward my master’s degree in applied statistics, I interned at the Tobacco Research Lab at NYU’s College of Global Public Health. During my internship, I collaborated with another researcher

Population Assessment of Tobacco and Health (PATH) Survey

The PATH study is a longitudinal study of tobacco use and attitudes funded by the National Institute of Health (NIH) and the Food and Drug Administation (FDA). The study began in 2013 and has aimed to follow the same respondents across multiple waves. The fourth and most recent wave ended in January of 2018. According to the FDA’s website, one of the goals of the study is to “study patterns of tobacco product use, cessation, and relapse” , which will be the focus of this blog post.

States of Tobacco Use

The PATH survey offers a number way to classify smokers. For my internship, we focused on five states of cigarette smoking/cessation.

  1. Current Established Smoker
  2. Former Established Smoker
  3. Current Experimental Smoker
  4. Former Experimental Smoker
  5. Non Smoker

The non-smoker category is self-explanatory, but the other ones require some explanation.

Established vs. Experimental Smokers

Established smokers were classified as respondents who reported to have smoked at least 100 cigarettes in their lifetime. Experimental smokers were those who reported smoking between 1 and 99 cigarettes in their lifetime.

Current vs. Former

Current smokers were those who smoked reported currently smoking everyday or some days. Former smokers were those who had reported smoking at least one cigarette in their lifetime but not smoking everyday or somedays currently.

Note on categories

not all studies use these five categories. For example, some studies focus on quit rates and define quitting by some threshold such as having not smoked any cigarettes in the past 30 days. Also note that the 100 cigarette threshold between established vs. experimental smokers is somewhat arbitrary. However, using a consistent threshold across wave allows us to make comparison and study transitions over time.

Our Goal

Our first goal was to visualize these transitions across the three waves of data that were available at the time my internship.
